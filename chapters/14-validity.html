14  Validity and Causal Reasoning

Throughout this book, you have loaded data, summarized it, visualized it, and modeled relationships. This final content chapter addresses the most consequential question in data analysis: does the pattern you found reflect a real causal mechanism, or is it an artifact of how the data was collected, analyzed, or presented?

This chapter introduces types of research validity, counterfactual reasoning, and quasi-experimental designs. We will use a concrete example — whether cannabis legalization in Colorado reduced opioid overdose deaths — to illustrate how these concepts shape the way you analyze data and communicate findings. The analysis draws on data from the CDC’s WONDER database and the research design frameworks from Freese and Kevern’s Handbook of Causal Analysis for Social Research and Reichardt’s entry on internal validity in the International Encyclopedia of the Social & Behavioral Sciences.

At first glance, Colorado’s rate appears to flatten around 2014 while the national average continues to climb. But is this evidence of a causal effect?

14.4 Types of Validity

14.4.1 Internal validity

Internal validity asks: did the treatment (cannabis legalization) cause the observed outcome (change in opioid deaths)? Threats to internal validity include confounding variables (other things that changed around 2014), selection bias (Colorado is not a random state), and maturation (trends that would have occurred anyway regardless of legalization).

14.4.2 External validity

External validity asks: would the same effect occur in other states? Even if legalization truly reduced opioid deaths in Colorado, the effect might not generalize to states with different demographics, healthcare systems, or drug markets.

14.4.3 Construct validity

Construct validity asks: are we measuring what we think we’re measuring? “Drug-induced deaths” is a broad category that includes many substances beyond opioids. Changes in coding practices, reporting requirements, or medical examiner staffing could affect the count without any real change in behavior.

14.5 Single-Group Pre/Post Design

The simplest design compares Colorado’s rates before and after legalization.

co_cod_df = cod_df[(cod_df['State'] == 'CO') & (cod_df['Year'] >= 2008)]

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 6))
sb.boxplot(x='Year', y='Rate', data=co_cod_df, ax=ax, color='green')
ax.axvline(5.5, color='r', ls='--', zorder=2, label='Legalization (2014)')
ax.axhline(
    co_cod_df.loc[co_cod_df['Year'] == 2014, 'Rate'].mean(),
    color='r', ls='--', zorder=2
)
ax.set_ylim((0, 50))
ax.legend()
co_before = co_cod_df.loc[co_cod_df['Year'].isin(range(2008, 2014)), 'Rate']
co_after = co_cod_df.loc[co_cod_df['Year'].isin(range(2015, 2021)), 'Rate']

print(f"Before mean: {co_before.mean():.1f}")
print(f"After mean: {co_after.mean():.1f}")
print(f"Difference: {co_after.mean() - co_before.mean():.1f}")

stats.ttest_ind(co_after, co_before, equal_var=False)
WarningThe fatal flaw of pre/post designs

Even if the difference is statistically significant, we cannot attribute it to legalization. Many other things changed between 2008–2013 and 2014–2020: the opioid crisis intensified nationally, fentanyl entered the drug supply, and Medicaid expansion occurred. Without a comparison group, we cannot distinguish the effect of legalization from the effect of time.

14.6.2 Compare changes across states

for name, before, after in [('Colorado', co_before_df, co_after_df),
                             ('Montana', mt_before_df, mt_after_df),
                             ('Delaware', de_before_df, de_after_df)]:

    t, p = stats.ttest_ind(after, before, equal_var=False)
    print(f"{name}: before {before.mean():.1f}, after {after.mean():.1f}, "
          f"diff {diff:+.1f}, t={t:.2f}, p={p:.4f}")

Montana’s increase is non-significant (p > 0.05) while Delaware’s increase is highly significant (p < 0.001), reflecting the fentanyl crisis that hit East Coast states particularly hard.

14.7 Counterfactual Reasoning

The core challenge of causal inference is that we can never observe the counterfactual — what would have happened to Colorado if it had not legalized cannabis. The comparison group serves as a proxy for this counterfactual, but it is only as good as the match between the treatment and comparison groups.

This is why randomized controlled experiments are the gold standard: random assignment ensures comparability on all characteristics, observed and unobserved. Observational studies like ours can never fully eliminate confounding.

14.8 Difference-in-Differences

A common quasi-experimental approach is difference-in-differences (DiD): compute the before-to-after change in the treatment group and subtract the before-to-after change in the comparison group.

co_change = co_after_df.mean() - co_before_df.mean()
mt_change = mt_after_df.mean() - mt_before_df.mean()

did = co_change - mt_change

print(f"Colorado change: {co_change:+.1f}")
print(f"Montana change: {mt_change:+.1f}")
print(f"Difference-in-differences: {did:+.1f}")

A negative DiD estimate would suggest that Colorado’s rate increased less (or decreased more) than Montana’s, consistent with a protective effect. But even this design relies on the parallel trends assumption — that Colorado and Montana would have followed the same trajectory in the absence of legalization.

14.9 Communicating Causal Claims

When you present findings from observational data, your language must reflect the limitations of your design.

Don’t write: “Cannabis legalization reduced opioid deaths in Colorado.”

Write instead: “Colorado’s drug-induced death rate increased less than comparable non-legalization states between 2008 and 2020, though this cross-state comparison cannot definitively attribute the difference to legalization.”

This is not hedging for its own sake — it is honest communication about what the evidence can and cannot support. As discussed in Chapter 11, your credibility depends on being clear about the boundaries of your knowledge.

NoteExercise 1: Identify threats to validity

List three specific threats to internal validity for the cannabis-opioid analysis. For each, explain how it could bias the results in either direction.

Solution:

  1. Confounding with Medicaid expansion: Colorado expanded Medicaid in 2014, the same year as legalization. Increased access to addiction treatment could reduce overdose deaths independently of cannabis access, inflating the apparent protective effect.

  2. Changes in drug supply: The rise of fentanyl accelerated after 2014, disproportionately affecting some states. If fentanyl reached Colorado later than comparison states, the apparent protective effect would be overstated.

  3. Reporting changes: Improvements in medical examiner toxicology testing could change how deaths are classified. A state that began testing for more substances might reclassify deaths from “drug-induced” to more specific categories, artificially reducing the count.

NoteExercise 2: Choose better comparison states

Using the state_rate_pivot data, identify the three states whose pre-2014 death rates most closely match Colorado’s. Plot their trajectories alongside Colorado’s. Does the visual evidence support or undermine the legalization hypothesis?

Solution:

co_pre = state_rate_pivot.loc['CO', 2008:2013].mean()
distances = (state_rate_pivot.loc[:, 2008:2013].mean(1) - co_pre).abs()
best_matches = distances.drop(['CO', 'WA']).sort_values().head(3).index.tolist()

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 6))
state_rate_pivot.loc[['CO'] + best_matches, :].T.plot(ax=ax, linewidth=2)
ax.axvline(2014, color='black', linestyle=':')
ax.set_ylabel('Deaths per 100,000')
ax.set_title('Colorado vs. Best-Matched Comparison States')
ax.legend()
NoteExercise 3: Compute a difference-in-differences

Using the comparison states you identified in Exercise 2, compute the difference-in-differences estimate for each pair (Colorado vs. each comparison state). Are the estimates consistent across comparisons?

Solution:

co_change = co_after_df.mean() - co_before_df.mean()

for state in best_matches:
    before = cod_df.loc[
        (cod_df['State'] == state) & (cod_df['Year'] >= 2008) & (cod_df['Year'] < 2014), 'Rate'
    ]
    after = cod_df.loc[
        (cod_df['State'] == state) & (cod_df['Year'] > 2014), 'Rate'
    ]
    change = after.mean() - before.mean()
    did = co_change - change
    print(f"CO vs {state}: DiD = {did:+.1f}")

If the DiD estimates are consistent (all negative, or all positive, or all near zero), the finding is more robust. If they vary widely, the result is sensitive to the choice of comparison group — a red flag.

```{python}
    t, p = stats.ttest_ind(after, before, equal_var=False)
    print(f"{name}: before {before.mean():.1f}, after {after.mean():.1f}, "
          f"diff {diff:+.1f}, t={t:.2f}, p={p:.4f}")
```

Montana's increase is non-significant (p > 0.05) while Delaware's increase is highly significant (p < 0.001), reflecting the fentanyl crisis that hit East Coast states particularly hard.

## Counterfactual Reasoning

The core challenge of causal inference is that we can never observe the **counterfactual** — what *would have happened* to Colorado if it had *not* legalized cannabis. The comparison group serves as a proxy for this counterfactual, but it is only as good as the match between the treatment and comparison groups.

This is why randomized controlled experiments are the gold standard: random assignment ensures comparability on all characteristics, observed and unobserved. Observational studies like ours can never fully eliminate confounding.

## Difference-in-Differences

A common quasi-experimental approach is **difference-in-differences** (DiD): compute the before-to-after change in the treatment group and subtract the before-to-after change in the comparison group.

```{python}
co_change = co_after_df.mean() - co_before_df.mean()
mt_change = mt_after_df.mean() - mt_before_df.mean()

did = co_change - mt_change

print(f"Colorado change: {co_change:+.1f}")
print(f"Montana change: {mt_change:+.1f}")
print(f"Difference-in-differences: {did:+.1f}")
```

A negative DiD estimate would suggest that Colorado's rate increased less (or decreased more) than Montana's, consistent with a protective effect. But even this design relies on the **parallel trends assumption** — that Colorado and Montana would have followed the same trajectory in the absence of legalization.

## Communicating Causal Claims

When you present findings from observational data, your language must reflect the limitations of your design.

**Don't write:** "Cannabis legalization reduced opioid deaths in Colorado."

**Write instead:** "Colorado's drug-induced death rate increased less than comparable non-legalization states between 2008 and 2020, though this cross-state comparison cannot definitively attribute the difference to legalization."

This is not hedging for its own sake — it is honest communication about what the evidence can and cannot support. As discussed in @sec-uncertainty, your credibility depends on being clear about the boundaries of your knowledge.

## Exercise 1: Identify threats to validity

List three specific threats to internal validity for the cannabis-opioid analysis. For each, explain how it could bias the results in either direction.

**Solution:**

1. **Confounding with Medicaid expansion:** Colorado expanded Medicaid in 2014, the same year as legalization. Increased access to addiction treatment could reduce overdose deaths independently of cannabis access, inflating the apparent protective effect.

2. **Changes in drug supply:** The rise of fentanyl accelerated after 2014, disproportionately affecting some states. If fentanyl reached Colorado later than comparison states, the apparent protective effect would be overstated.

3. **Reporting changes:** Improvements in medical examiner toxicology testing could change how deaths are classified. A state that began testing for more substances might reclassify deaths from "drug-induced" to more specific categories, artificially reducing the count.

Using the comparison states you identified in Exercise 2, compute the difference-in-differences estimate for each pair (Colorado vs. each comparison state). Are the estimates consistent across comparisons?
