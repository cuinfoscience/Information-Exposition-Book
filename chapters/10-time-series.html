10  Time Series Visualization and Patterns

In Chapter 9 you learned to parse, index, and slice temporal data. This chapter puts those skills to work on a core challenge of data analysis: making sense of noisy temporal data. Raw time series are often too volatile to interpret — daily temperatures swing wildly, stock prices jitter, and taxi pickups spike and plummet. The signal is buried in the noise.

Temporal data is rarely random and uncorrelated. There are often consistent patterns in human and natural behavior that recur at various frequencies: hourly circadian rhythms, weekly work-weekend cycles, monthly billing cycles, and annual seasonality. Identifying and understanding these underlying temporal patterns is crucial for describing behavior, characterizing anomalies, and forecasting future behavior.

This chapter introduces techniques for revealing these patterns: resampling, rolling averages, boxplot summaries, temporal pattern decomposition across frequencies, and formal time series decomposition.

10.3 Visualizing Raw Time Series

Start by plotting the raw temperature data. With over 50,000 daily observations spanning 140+ years, the result is dense and hard to interpret.

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(18, 6))

temp_df['AvgTemp'].plot(ax=ax)
ax.set_ylim((0, 16))
ax.set_ylabel('Temperature (°C)')
ax.set_title('Average daily temperature 1880–2024')

Is there a warming trend? Hard to say with this much daily variation. We need to smooth the data.

10.4 Resampling

Resampling changes the frequency of your time series. You can use pd.Grouper inside .groupby() or the .resample() method to aggregate from daily to monthly, annual, or any other frequency.

10.4.1 Sampling specific dates

Try resampling to just July 1 of each year. The temperature on a single day is not a reliable indicator of a warming pattern.

july_firsts = pd.date_range(
    start='1880-07-01',
    end='2022-07-01',
    freq=pd.DateOffset(years=1)
)

july_first_temps = temp_df.loc[july_firsts]

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(18, 6))
july_first_temps['AvgTemp'].plot(ax=ax)
ax.set_ylim((6, 12))
ax.set_ylabel('Temperature (°C)')
ax.set_title('July 1 temperatures 1880–2022')
ax.axhline(8.59, c='k', ls='--', lw=.5)

10.4.2 Monthly averages

Averaging all days in each month is more robust than sampling a single day.

monthly_avg_temps = temp_df.groupby(pd.Grouper(freq='MS')).agg({'AvgTemp': 'mean'})

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(18, 6))
monthly_avg_temps['AvgTemp'].plot(ax=ax)
ax.set_ylabel('Temperature (°C)')
ax.set_title('Average monthly temperature 1880–2024')
ax.axhline(8.59, c='k', ls='--', lw=.5)
ax.set_ylim((6, 12))

10.4.3 Annual averages

Aggregating to annual averages removes the seasonal cycle entirely and reveals the long-term trend.

annual_avg_temps = temp_df.groupby(pd.Grouper(freq='YS')).agg({'AvgTemp': 'mean'})

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(18, 6))
annual_avg_temps['AvgTemp'].plot(ax=ax)
ax.set_ylabel('Temperature (°C)')
ax.set_title('Average annual temperature 1880–2024')
ax.axhline(8.59, c='k', ls='--', lw=.5)
ax.set_ylim((6, 12))

The warming trend that was invisible in the daily data is now unmistakable. This illustrates a fundamental trade-off: smoothing reveals trends but removes detail.

10.5 Rolling Averages

Unlike resampling, which bins data into discrete periods, rolling averages produce a value for every data point, making them useful for overlaying on the original data.

quarterly_rollmean = temp_df['AvgTemp'].rolling(90).mean()

decadal_rollmean = temp_df['AvgTemp'].rolling(3650).mean()

quarterly_rollmean.plot(ax=ax, color='tab:blue', label='Quarterly')

ax.set_title('Average daily temperature 1880–2024')
ax.legend(loc='upper left')

ax.set_ylim((6, 12))

temps_1984 = temp_df.loc['1984', 'AvgTemp']

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 6))

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(2, 6))

temps_since_1970 = temp_df.loc['1970':, :]

10.7 Temporal Patterns at Different Frequencies

One of the most useful applications of temporal data analysis is identifying recurring patterns at different time scales. Let’s systematically examine patterns at the hourly, daily, weekly, monthly, and annual level.

10.7.1 Hourly patterns: Circadian rhythms

Most people in a given location sleep at roughly the same time. When people are asleep, activity drops, and this should be visible in the data.

sb.catplot(
    x='hour',
    y='yellowcab',
    data=nyc_df,
    kind='point',
    aspect=2
)

Taxi pickups are lowest around 4–5 AM and peak during the evening rush.

10.7.2 Weekly patterns: Workdays vs. weekends

We can make a heatmap to show the interaction between hour of day and day of week.

rideshare_pivot = pd.pivot_table(
    nyc_df,
    index='hour',
    columns='weekday',
    values='yellowcab',
    aggfunc='sum'
)

rideshare_pivot = rideshare_pivot.loc[:, weekday_order]

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(4, 6))
sb.heatmap(rideshare_pivot, ax=ax, square=True)
f.tight_layout()

The heatmap reveals that weekday patterns (morning and evening peaks) differ from weekend patterns (later starts, more nightlife activity). Different datasets show different weekly patterns.

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 4))
sb.pointplot(x='weekday', y='Volume', data=sp500_df, order=weekday_order)
f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 4))
sb.pointplot(x='weekday', y='AvgTemp', data=temp_df, order=weekday_order)
TipThe weekday temperature “pattern”

If you see a tiny weekly pattern in global temperature data, pause before concluding that the weather follows a weekly cycle. Any apparent pattern is likely an artifact of the data processing, measurement timing, or station distribution — not a real physical phenomenon. Critical thinking about spurious patterns is essential.

10.7.4 Annual patterns: Seasonality

Seasonality is one of the most common temporal patterns. Temperature has an obvious annual cycle, but many human activities do as well.

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 4))
sb.pointplot(x='month', y='AvgTemp', data=temp_df)
f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 4))
sb.pointplot(x='month', y='Total', data=passengers_df)

DIA passenger traffic shows a strong seasonal pattern: summer months (June–August) are busiest, with a secondary peak in March (spring break).

10.8 Decomposing Time Series

A more formal approach to separating trend, seasonal, and residual components uses statistical decomposition. The statsmodels library provides seasonal_decompose, which breaks a time series into three additive (or multiplicative) components: trend, seasonal pattern, and residual noise.

import statsmodels.api as sm
f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 4))
passengers_df['Total'].plot(ax=ax)
ax.set_ylabel('Total passengers')
ax.set_title('Monthly passenger traffic through DIA')

A 12-month rolling average removes the seasonal signal to reveal the trend.

f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 6))

passengers_df['Total'].plot(ax=ax)
passengers_df['Total'].rolling(12).mean().plot(ax=ax, c='tab:green', lw=2)

ax.set_ylabel('Total passengers')
ax.set_title('Monthly DIA traffic with 12-month rolling average')

10.8.1 Formal decomposition

The seasonal_decompose function produces four panels: the observed data, the estimated trend, the seasonal component, and the residual.

decomposition = sm.tsa.seasonal_decompose(passengers_df['Total'], model='additive')

plt.rcParams["figure.figsize"] = (8, 8)
f = decomposition.plot()

for _ax in f.axes:
    _ax.axvline(pd.Timestamp('2001-09-11'), color='r', ls='--', lw=1, zorder=0)
    _ax.axvline(pd.Timestamp('2020-03-11'), color='r', ls='--', lw=1, zorder=0)

The trend component shows steady growth interrupted by 9/11 and COVID-19. The seasonal component shows the consistent summer peak and winter trough. The residual captures everything the trend and seasonal components do not explain — including the massive COVID shock.

10.8.2 Linear modeling of trend

You can also model the long-term trend with a linear regression, as introduced in Chapter 13. First, create a numeric feature for months since the airport opened.

passengers_df['months_since_opening'] = (
    passengers_df.index - pd.Timestamp('1995-01-01')
) / pd.Timedelta(30, 'D')

g = sb.lmplot(
    x='months_since_opening',
    y='Total',
    data=passengers_df,
    aspect=2,
    ci=0,
    scatter_kws={'color': 'k', 's': 25},
    line_kws={'lw': 5, 'color': 'r', 'zorder': 0}
)

The raw data has enormous variance because of seasonality. Smoothing first with a 12-month rolling average and then fitting a linear model produces a much cleaner trend estimate.

passengers_df['Total_smoothed'] = passengers_df['Total'].rolling(12).mean()

g = sb.lmplot(
    x='months_since_opening',
    y='Total_smoothed',
    data=passengers_df,
    aspect=2,
    ci=0,
    scatter_kws={'color': 'k', 's': 25},
    line_kws={'lw': 5, 'color': 'r', 'zorder': 0}
)

## Box Plots for Temporal Distributions

## Temporal Patterns at Different Frequencies

One of the most useful applications of temporal data analysis is identifying recurring patterns at different time scales. Let's systematically examine patterns at the hourly, daily, weekly, monthly, and annual level.

### Hourly patterns: Circadian rhythms

Most people in a given location sleep at roughly the same time. When people are asleep, activity drops, and this should be visible in the data.

```{python}
sb.catplot(
    x='hour',
    y='yellowcab',
    data=nyc_df,
    kind='point',
    aspect=2
)
```

Taxi pickups are lowest around 4–5 AM and peak during the evening rush.

### Weekly patterns: Workdays vs. weekends

We can make a heatmap to show the interaction between hour of day and day of week.

rideshare_pivot = rideshare_pivot.loc[:, weekday_order]

The heatmap reveals that weekday patterns (morning and evening peaks) differ from weekend patterns (later starts, more nightlife activity). Different datasets show different weekly patterns.

## The weekday temperature "pattern"
If you see a tiny weekly pattern in global temperature data, pause before concluding that the weather follows a weekly cycle. Any apparent pattern is likely an artifact of the data processing, measurement timing, or station distribution — not a real physical phenomenon. Critical thinking about spurious patterns is essential.
:::

## Decomposing Time Series

A more formal approach to separating trend, seasonal, and residual components uses statistical decomposition. The `statsmodels` library provides `seasonal_decompose`, which breaks a time series into three additive (or multiplicative) components: trend, seasonal pattern, and residual noise.

```{python}
import statsmodels.api as sm
```

```{python}
f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 4))
passengers_df['Total'].plot(ax=ax)
ax.set_ylabel('Total passengers')
ax.set_title('Monthly passenger traffic through DIA')
```

A 12-month rolling average removes the seasonal signal to reveal the trend.

```{python}
f, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 6))

passengers_df['Total'].plot(ax=ax)
passengers_df['Total'].rolling(12).mean().plot(ax=ax, c='tab:green', lw=2)

ax.set_ylabel('Total passengers')
ax.set_title('Monthly DIA traffic with 12-month rolling average')
```

### Formal decomposition

The `seasonal_decompose` function produces four panels: the observed data, the estimated trend, the seasonal component, and the residual.

```{python}
decomposition = sm.tsa.seasonal_decompose(passengers_df['Total'], model='additive')

plt.rcParams["figure.figsize"] = (8, 8)
f = decomposition.plot()

for _ax in f.axes:
    _ax.axvline(pd.Timestamp('2001-09-11'), color='r', ls='--', lw=1, zorder=0)
    _ax.axvline(pd.Timestamp('2020-03-11'), color='r', ls='--', lw=1, zorder=0)
```

The trend component shows steady growth interrupted by 9/11 and COVID-19. The seasonal component shows the consistent summer peak and winter trough. The residual captures everything the trend and seasonal components do not explain — including the massive COVID shock.

### Linear modeling of trend

You can also model the long-term trend with a linear regression, as introduced in @sec-linear-models. First, create a numeric feature for months since the airport opened.

```{python}
passengers_df['months_since_opening'] = (
    passengers_df.index - pd.Timestamp('1995-01-01')
) / pd.Timedelta(30, 'D')

g = sb.lmplot(
    x='months_since_opening',
    y='Total',
    data=passengers_df,
    aspect=2,
    ci=0,
    scatter_kws={'color': 'k', 's': 25},
    line_kws={'lw': 5, 'color': 'r', 'zorder': 0}
)
```

The raw data has enormous variance because of seasonality. Smoothing first with a 12-month rolling average and then fitting a linear model produces a much cleaner trend estimate.

```{python}
passengers_df['Total_smoothed'] = passengers_df['Total'].rolling(12).mean()

g = sb.lmplot(
    x='months_since_opening',
    y='Total_smoothed',
    data=passengers_df,
    aspect=2,
    ci=0,
    scatter_kws={'color': 'k', 's': 25},
    line_kws={'lw': 5, 'color': 'r', 'zorder': 0}
)
```
